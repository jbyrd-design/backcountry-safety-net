Trail Description

Lost Creek Trail #629 is the primary access route into the Lost Creek Wilderness, providing hikers with access to the unique granite rock formations and pristine creek valleys that characterize this wilderness area. The trail follows Lost Creek through a narrow canyon with towering rock walls, offering spectacular scenery and opportunities for wilderness camping.

The trail begins at the Lost Creek Trailhead (USFS Trailhead #629) and follows an old roadbed for the first mile before entering the wilderness boundary. The path then follows Lost Creek upstream, crossing the creek multiple times. During spring runoff (typically late May through mid-June), these crossings can be challenging and potentially dangerous.

Key landmarks along the route include:

  • Mile 1.2: Lost Creek Wilderness Boundary
  • Mile 2.5: Junction with Goose Creek Trail #631
  • Mile 4.8: Junction with McCurdy Trail #607
  • Mile 8.5: Lost Creek Lakes area (popular camping destination)

Best Seasons & Current Conditions

Peak Season: July through September (after snowmelt, before first snow)

Spring (May-June): Creek crossings dangerous due to runoff; snow possible at higher elevations

Summer (July-Aug): Best conditions; afternoon thunderstorms common (start early)

Fall (Sept-Oct): Excellent hiking; aspens changing; prepare for cold nights

Winter (Nov-Apr): Trail not maintained; requires snowshoes; high avalanche risk

Wilderness Regulations & Leave No Trace

Required Practices

  • Group size limit: 12 people maximum
  • Stock limit: Heart bar limitation in effect
  • Camping: 200 feet from lakes, streams, and trails
  • Fires: Use existing fire rings; collect dead and down wood only
  • Human waste: Pack out toilet paper; bury waste 6-8 inches deep, 200 ft from water
  • Food storage: Bear canisters or hang method (minimum 10 ft high, 4 ft from trunk)
  • Stock: Certified weed-free feed required 72 hours before entry
  • Dogs: Allowed on leash; pack out waste

Source: Lost Creek Wilderness Regulations, Pike-San Isabel National Forest

Recommended Equipment & Preparation

Essential Gear

  • Navigation: Topo map, compass, GPS
  • Water: 3L capacity + filter/purification
  • Food: Extra day's supply
  • Clothing: Rain gear, insulation layer
  • First aid kit with blister treatment
  • Emergency shelter (space blanket minimum)
  • Headlamp with extra batteries
  • Fire starter and matches (waterproof)

Weather Considerations

  • Afternoon thunderstorms common June-August
  • Start by 6-7 AM to descend by noon
  • Lightning danger above treeline
  • Temperature can drop 30°F overnight
  • Snow possible any month of year
  • Check forecast: weather.gov/bou
